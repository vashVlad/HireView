## THE TARGET INDUSTRIES

These five industries are the highest-opportunity targets based on 2026 market research from McKinsey, PwC, Gartner, and Forrester. They share three traits: high volume of repetitive manual work, clear measurable bottlenecks, and willingness to pay for solutions that prove ROI. Most enterprises automate less than 20% of their workflows — the gap is enormous.

### INDUSTRY 1 — RECRUITING & HR OPERATIONS
**Why it's wide open:** Recruiting is manual by default. Resume review, candidate sourcing, interview scheduling, and decision-making are all done by hand. Most companies have no measurement of recruiting efficiency. AI can compress timelines by 30–60% and improve match quality significantly.

**Specific problems everywhere:**
- Manual resume screening consuming 5–10 hours per open role
- Imprecise candidate sourcing producing low-quality pipelines
- No structured evaluation of candidate fit against role requirements
- Interview feedback scattered across emails and notes with no synthesis
- Average time-to-hire stretching to 42 days for skilled positions
- Hiring managers and recruiters misaligned on what good looks like

**Vlad's existing proof:** HireView at Brillio — resume screener + job description analyzer — 60% throughput improvement validated in live use.

**Opportunity size:** Every company with more than 10 employees is recruiting. Every staffing agency, every HR consultancy, every internal talent team has this problem right now.

### INDUSTRY 2 — LEGAL OPERATIONS & LAW FIRMS
**Why it's wide open:** Legal work is knowledge-intensive and almost entirely manual. Contract review, due diligence, compliance checking, and document drafting consume enormous amounts of billable time that AI can dramatically compress. Legal is one of the top four highest-ROI industries for AI according to McKinsey and Forrester.

**Specific problems everywhere:**
- Contract review taking days when AI can surface key clauses in minutes
- Due diligence on deals requiring weeks of manual document analysis
- Compliance monitoring done manually with high error rate
- Client intake and onboarding inconsistent across team members
- Billing narratives written manually for every matter
- Knowledge trapped in individual lawyers, not in searchable systems

**Opportunity:** Build AI tools for small to mid-size law firms (10–100 attorneys) who cannot afford enterprise legal tech but desperately need it. Contract summarizers, intake automation, compliance checkers, matter status dashboards.

**Why this fits Vlad's methodology:** Legal firms are exactly like Quinn's — small teams with no purpose-built tools, doing everything manually, with clear bottlenecks that a focused AI solution can eliminate.

### INDUSTRY 3 — HEALTHCARE ADMINISTRATION
**Why it's wide open:** Healthcare professionals lose 34% of their time on administrative tasks that could be automated (Statista 2026). AI in healthcare delivers $3.20 return for every $1 invested within 14 months. The bottleneck is not clinical care — it is the operational layer around it.

**Specific problems everywhere:**
- Prior authorization requests taking days, causing treatment delays
- Patient intake forms and data entry done manually
- Insurance verification requiring phone calls and hold times
- Appointment scheduling and follow-up coordination done by hand
- Medical billing and coding errors causing claim denials
- Documentation compliance monitoring done manually

**Opportunity:** Target small to mid-size medical practices (5–50 providers) — clinics, dental practices, physical therapy offices, specialty practices. They have the same problems as hospitals but no budget for enterprise solutions. AI tools that handle intake, scheduling, insurance verification, and billing coordination have immediate measurable impact.

**Important note:** Healthcare has HIPAA compliance requirements. Solutions must be designed with data privacy from day one. This is a moat — it keeps competitors out and makes validated solutions more valuable.

### INDUSTRY 4 — LOGISTICS & SUPPLY CHAIN OPERATIONS
**Why it's wide open:** 61% of manufacturing executives report decreased costs as a direct result of AI in supply chain (McKinsey 2026). Modern supply chains span continents and dozens of stakeholders — even minor delays ripple across the entire chain. Most small logistics operators and freight brokers still coordinate by email and spreadsheet.

**Specific problems everywhere:**
- Shipment tracking done manually across multiple carrier portals
- Exception management (delays, damages, missing items) handled reactively
- Vendor communication unstructured and inconsistent
- Inventory visibility non-existent until stockouts occur
- Route optimization not happening — drivers use default GPS
- Invoice reconciliation taking days per billing cycle

**Opportunity:** Target small logistics operators, freight brokers, and regional distributors (20–200 employees). They have the same operational complexity as enterprise logistics companies but no dedicated operations technology. AI tools for shipment visibility, exception alerts, vendor communication, and invoice processing have immediate ROI.

**Why this fits Vlad's methodology:** FlowOps was built for a similar problem — physical operations with no purpose-built coordination system. The same pattern applies here at larger scale.

**Why it's wide open:** Professional services firms run on knowledge work that is poorly systematized. Proposals, client onboarding, project delivery, and billing are all inconsistent. These firms are large enough to feel the pain but small enough to have no dedicated operations team to fix it.

**Opportunity:** Staffing agencies, boutique consulting firms, accounting practices, and marketing agencies (10–100 employees) all have these problems. AI tools for proposal generation, onboarding automation, project tracking, and knowledge management have clear ROI and short sales cycles because the pain is visible and measurable.

**Vlad's existing proof:** HireView solves a staffing/recruiting operations problem. The same methodology applies across the full professional services workflow.

## THE PATTERN THAT SELLS

Research from McKinsey, PwC, and Gartner confirms the same pattern across every winning AI implementation in 2026:

**The solutions that sell are not the most technically impressive. They are the ones that:**
1. Target one high-friction workflow with clear before/after measurement
2. Deliver ROI in 30–90 days, not 6–12 months
3. Are built by someone who did the work themselves before automating it
4. Produce a number — time saved, errors reduced, cost per transaction reduced
5. Require no enterprise IT involvement to implement

This is exactly Vlad's methodology. He does not build platforms. He identifies one bottleneck, builds the minimum solution that removes it, validates the number, and moves to the next one.

## THE METHODOLOGY — 5 PHASES

Every project follows this sequence. AI agents must never skip phases.

---

### PHASE 1 — EMBED & OBSERVE
**Principle:** Do the work before building anything. You cannot design a solution for a problem you haven't felt yourself.

**Steps:**
1. Take on the actual role or responsibility inside the organization
2. Map the full workflow end to end — every step, every handoff, every decision point
3. Identify where time is lost, where errors happen, where people work around the system
4. Talk to the people doing the work — what frustrates them most?
5. Do not propose any solution yet

**Output:** A written workflow map with bottlenecks marked and prioritized by impact.

**Key question:** What is the single highest-leverage bottleneck — the one constraint that, if removed, changes everything?

**Reference:** The Goal (Goldratt) — every system has one bottleneck. Fix the bottleneck, not the symptoms.

---

### PHASE 2 — DEFINE THE PROBLEM
**Principle:** A problem well defined is half solved. Most people skip this phase and build the wrong thing.

**Steps:**
1. Write one sentence describing the problem in business terms, not technical terms
2. Quantify the current state — how long does it take? How many errors? How much does it cost?
3. Define what success looks like — what will be measurably different after the solution exists?
4. Confirm the problem with at least one other person inside the organization
5. Write a one-paragraph problem statement a non-technical stakeholder can understand

**Output:** A signed-off problem statement with current state metrics and success criteria.

**Key question:** If I solve this, what number changes — and by how much?

**Reference:** The Pyramid Principle (Minto) — lead with the conclusion. State the problem, state the impact, state the solution. Never bury the point.

---

### PHASE 3 — DESIGN THE SOLUTION
**Principle:** Design for the minimum viable fix. Build the smallest thing that removes the bottleneck.

**Steps:**
1. List every possible approach to solving the problem
2. Eliminate anything that takes more than 2 weeks to prototype
3. Select the approach with the highest impact and lowest complexity
4. Sketch the solution before writing any code or prompts
5. Define the user flow — who uses it, when, and what they do
6. Identify which AI capabilities are needed (summarization, classification, extraction, generation, search)
7. Get informal sign-off from one stakeholder before building

**Output:** A one-page solution design with user flow, AI capability requirements, and build timeline.

**Key question:** What is the smallest version of this solution that would still change how the team works?

**Reference:** High Output Management (Grove) — design solutions that multiply what the team can do, not replace what they do.

---

### PHASE 4 — BUILD & ITERATE
**Principle:** Ship something people can touch in under 2 weeks. Real feedback from real use is worth more than any amount of planning.

**Steps:**
1. Build the minimum version first — one feature, one workflow, one user type
2. Use AI (Claude) as the core engine wherever possible
3. Test it yourself first using real data from the organization
4. Give it to one person inside the organization and watch them use it without explaining anything
5. Note every point of confusion, error, or friction
6. Fix the highest-impact issues before expanding scope
7. Log every build session in the Development Log

**Output:** A working prototype used by at least one real person on a real task, with a Development Log entry for every session.

**Key question:** Does this actually change how someone works, or does it just feel useful?

**Tools:** Claude AI (Anthropic API), Supabase, Next.js, React, TypeScript, Tailwind, Framer Motion, Zustand.

**Reference:** The Phoenix Project (Gene Kim) — flow is everything. Remove the work that slows the system. Ship small, ship fast, learn constantly.

---

### PHASE 5 — VALIDATE & DOCUMENT
**Principle:** A result without a number is an opinion. Always measure before and after.

**Steps:**
1. Measure the before state — time, volume, error rate, cost
2. Measure the after state using the same metric after at least one week of real use
3. Calculate the percentage improvement
4. Write the Impact Report (one page: problem → solution → result → number)
5. Update the Case Study with results, challenges, and lessons learned
6. Present results to at least one stakeholder inside the organization
7. Identify the next bottleneck to address

**Output:** An Impact Report with a validated percentage improvement and a stakeholder who can vouch for the result.

**Key question:** What specific number changed, and can someone else confirm it?

**Reference:** Measure What Matters (Doerr) — outcomes are what matter, not outputs.

## HOW TO QUANTIFY IMPACT

Every solution must have a dollar value attached. Use this formula:

**Time saved per person per week × hourly cost × number of people × 52 weeks = annual dollar value**

Example: HireView saves a recruiter 2 hours per week. Recruiter costs $35/hour. 1 recruiter.
Annual value = 2 × $35 × 52 = **$3,640/year** from one user. Team of 5 = **$18,200/year**.

Industry benchmarks to reference when making the case:
- AI workflow automation delivers 30–50% faster execution on average
- Cost reductions of 20–40% are typical in documented deployments
- Error rate reductions of up to 70% are common in document-heavy workflows
- Healthcare AI delivers $3.20 return per $1 invested within 14 months
- Legal AI compresses contract review from days to minutes

Always calculate this. Always include it in the Impact Report.

**Reference:** Financial Intelligence (Berman) — every operational improvement has a dollar value.

## HOW TO PRESENT TO STAKEHOLDERS

Use the Pyramid Principle structure for every recommendation:

1. **Lead with the conclusion** — "I found that X is costing the team Y hours per week. I built a solution that reduces this by Z%."
2. **Support with evidence** — show the before and after numbers
3. **Explain the solution** — brief, non-technical description of what you built
4. **State what you need** — time to expand it, access to more data, a second user to test with

Never lead with how you built it. Always lead with what it changed.

**Reference:** The Pyramid Principle (Minto) + Never Split the Difference (Voss)

## DOCUMENTATION SYSTEM

Every project maintains three living documents. AI agents must keep these updated.

### 1. Development Log
Updated every build session:
```
DATE: [date]
WHAT I BUILT OR CHANGED: [description]
WHY I MADE THIS DECISION: [reasoning]
PROBLEMS ENCOUNTERED: [issues]
HOW I SOLVED THEM: [solutions]
WHAT'S NEXT: [next step]
```

### 2. Impact Report (one page)
Updated after each validation cycle:
- Problem that existed before
- Solution built
- Measured result (percentage + dollar value)
- Current status
- Who can vouch for it (name, role, company)

### 3. Case Study
Updated after each major milestone:
- Problem framing (why this mattered to the business)
- Research and discovery (what you observed)
- Solution design (what you chose and why)
- Technical approach (how you built it)
- Results and validation (numbers)
- Challenges and lessons learned
- Phased roadmap (what comes next)

## INSTRUCTIONS FOR AI AGENTS (COWORK)

When assisting Vlad on any project:

1. **Always identify the phase first.** Before doing any work, state which phase of the methodology this task belongs to.

2. **Never skip phases.** If Vlad asks to build something before the problem is defined, push back. Ask: "Have we written the problem statement and defined success metrics yet?"

3. **Always push for measurement.** Every feature, every change, every iteration must have a measurable outcome attached. If there is no number, ask: "How will we know this worked?"

4. **Keep documentation updated.** After every build session, prompt Vlad to update the Development Log. After every validation, update the Impact Report.

5. **Think in business terms first.** Frame everything around operational impact, not technical implementation. The stakeholder doesn't care how it works. They care what changed.

6. **Challenge scope creep.** If new features are being added before the core solution is validated, flag it. Ask: "Has the minimum version been tested with real users yet?"

7. **Maintain the identity.** Every output must reflect Vlad's positioning: operations systems builder who applies AI to change how organizations work. Not a developer. Not a student. A consultant who builds.

8. **Always calculate dollar value.** When a percentage improvement is validated, calculate the annual dollar value using the formula above. This number goes in the Impact Report.
